Cultural Shame Concepts
in Indian Higher Education

Four culturally grounded concepts of shame, each with its own temporal orientation, social reference, and function in educational settings. Select a concept to read its vignette and analytical commentary.

Indian philosophical, religious, and social traditions have long embedded shame-related orientations within normative frameworks of moral conduct, collective belonging, and relational accountability. The four concepts presented here, lajjā, vṛīḍā, apatrāpya, and izzat, are not simply variants of a single phenomenon. Each operates through a distinct temporal logic, addresses a different social reference point, and carries its own configuration of inhibiting and generative potential in educational settings.

Lajjā designates a continuously active internalised moral orientation. Vṛīḍā arises retrospectively, after a situational boundary has been crossed. Apatrāpya operates ahead of the situation as anticipatory sensitivity to social evaluation. Izzat extends the frame beyond the individual to encompass family and community across time. The TRACE study data show broadly convergent gender patterns across all four concepts in the educational field, though subtle differences in emphasis persist, particularly regarding expressive restraint, anticipatory self-monitoring, and the relational scope of shame.

Lajjā  ·  लज्जा

Internalised Moral Self-Regulation

Temporal orientation: continuous  ·  Primary reference: internalised moral compass

Function Ethical self-guidance Educational manifestation Respectful, self-controlled conduct Gender pattern Convergent

Vignette

Priya sits in the front row of a mechanical engineering seminar. Professor Iyer asks students to critique a peer's design submission projected on screen. Several classmates speak freely, pointing out flaws with a directness that makes Priya uncomfortable. She has identified the same problems and could contribute, but something holds her back. It is not fear of being wrong. It is the sense that the tone in the room has shifted away from what she considers appropriate: speech without regard for the person whose work is being discussed. She waits. When she finally speaks, she frames her observation carefully, acknowledging what works before naming what does not. After class, a classmate asks why she held back so long. Priya finds the question difficult to answer. The restraint did not feel like a choice.

TRACE data show that female respondents score marginally higher on modesty items, whilst male respondents show slightly stronger alignment with moral orientation items. Neither difference reaches statistical significance, supporting lajjā as a gender-transcending ethical orientation.

Lajjā is a cultivated moral orientation that shapes conduct from within, continuously and independently of external observation.

Lajjā operates as a continuously active internalised orientation, present before, during, and after any specific situation. Priya's restraint arises from a cultivated sense of what appropriate conduct requires: an attentiveness to context, relationship, and the moral implications of speech. Bhawuk (2011) describes lajjā as an "internal governor" that guides individuals away from what is inappropriate and towards what is required. In educational settings, this manifests as embodied modesty, respectful self-awareness, and a readiness to hold individual expression within the bounds of relational accountability (Bhawuk, 2011, 2019; Misra & Mohanty, 2002).

Inhibiting dimension

  • Restraint may suppress analytically valuable contributions
  • Modesty norms can silence legitimate critical voice
  • Historically associated with gendered control of women's conduct
  • May reinforce hierarchical deference in ways that limit intellectual exchange

Resource dimension

  • Sustains ethical self-regulation independent of external enforcement
  • Supports respectful classroom interaction and social cohesion
  • Cultivates attentiveness to relational and contextual demands
  • Provides a moral basis for reflective, care-oriented learning practice

Vṛīḍā  ·  व्रीडा

Retrospective Restraint and Relational Withdrawal

Temporal orientation: retrospective  ·  Primary reference: situational relational restraint

Function Post-hoc withdrawal and remorse Educational manifestation Deference towards authority figures Gender pattern Convergent, slight female tendency towards hesitation

Vignette

During a tutorial, Nisha challenges Professor Mehta's explanation of a circuit diagram. She does so confidently, citing a source from her own reading. Professor Mehta pauses, then responds with measured formality: "That source is not on the syllabus." The correction is quiet, but the room shifts. Later, walking to the canteen, Nisha replays the moment. She is uncertain whether she was right to speak as she did. A feeling settles, closer to a wish that she had been more careful of the relationship. She regrets something about how she occupied the room.

TRACE data show female respondents report slightly higher hesitation to disagree with authority figures (M = 3.22 vs. 3.15 for males). Both values lie above the scale midpoint, indicating that vṛīḍā-related restraint is broadly shared across genders.

Vṛīḍā is a retrospective affective response to a perceived mismatch between one's conduct and the relational norms of the setting. It arises after the situation and concerns the quality of one's presence within a relationship, rather than the correctness of one's actions.

Vṛīḍā surfaces after the moment has passed, as a response to the perceived distance between how one acted and what the relational context required. Heim (2022) describes vṛīḍā as both "the sense of shame or moral disapprobation of an immoral action" and as "the shyness which is a cause of a young lady's excellence," a dual reading that captures its ambivalence: vṛīḍā sustains ethical sensitivity and relational care, and in hierarchical educational settings it also shapes the conditions under which voice and critical exchange are possible (Heim, 2022, pp. 322–323).

Inhibiting dimension

  • Retrospective shame may discourage future assertive participation
  • Reinforces deference norms in hierarchical teacher-student relations
  • May lead to over-correction and excessive self-monitoring
  • Historically used to constrain women's expressive freedom

Resource dimension

  • Supports ethical reflection after situational transgressions
  • Sustains relational sensitivity and awareness of authority dynamics
  • Motivates careful calibration of voice in complex social settings
  • Can be a basis for developing contextually attuned assertiveness

Apatrāpya  ·  अपत्रप्य

Anticipatory Avoidance of Social Censure

Temporal orientation: anticipatory  ·  Primary reference: social evaluation and censure

Function Preventive avoidance of disapproval Educational manifestation Behavioural adaptation to perceived norms Gender pattern Convergent, female tendency towards communal reference

Vignette

Rohan has not completed the assigned problem set. The solutions are half-finished in his notebook. As the tutorial begins, teaching assistant Dr. Pillai announces spot-checks. Rohan calculates quickly: if called upon, the incompleteness will be visible. He has prepared enough to navigate a basic question, but a follow-up would expose the gap. He decides to volunteer nothing. He also moves his notebook slightly off the desk. What he feels is a vigilant awareness that the eyes of others are upon him, and that being seen to fall short would cost him something he cannot easily name but does not want to lose. He gets through the session without incident. The feeling does not fully leave.

TRACE data show female respondents score slightly higher on communal reference items (M = 3.45 vs. 3.27 for males on "the opinion of the community is very important to me"). Across all apatrāpya items, both genders score above the midpoint, with no statistically significant differences.

Apatrāpya is a prospective sensitivity to social evaluation that shapes conduct before the situation unfolds, oriented towards the anticipated assessments of others and the communal standards they represent.

Apatrāpya operates ahead of the situation, as a sensitivity to the eyes of others that shapes conduct before any judgement is delivered. In Buddhist ethical psychology, the concept describes acute attentiveness to social censure, functioning as a preventive regulator against conduct that could attract disapproval (Heim, 2022, pp. 217–218). For Rohan, the exposure of incomplete work is a social risk: the possibility of being seen as someone who has fallen short of communal expectations. Apatrāpya-driven self-regulation is prospective, a continuous attentiveness to one's own conduct in relation to anticipated social evaluation (Rajaram, 2019; Gethin, 1998).

Inhibiting dimension

  • Persistent vigilance produces sustained cognitive and emotional load
  • May suppress genuine engagement in favour of impression management
  • Avoidance strategies reduce learning through active participation
  • Can consolidate risk-aversion and withdrawal from academic challenge

Resource dimension

  • Motivates preparation and attentiveness to communal norms
  • Supports adaptive self-monitoring in complex social settings
  • Builds sensitivity to how conduct is read by others, a form of social intelligence
  • Can function as a driver for meeting collective standards of conduct

Izzat  ·  इज़्ज़त

Collective Honour and Trans-temporal Responsibility

Temporal orientation: trans-temporal  ·  Primary reference: collective reputation

Function Familial and communal honour Educational manifestation Academic achievement as collective project Gender pattern Convergent, context-specific differences

Vignette

Vikram's results are due to be posted at noon. He has not slept well. It is not only his own standing that is at stake. His father took leave from work to attend the end-of-year ceremony. His older sister, who studied the same programme three years earlier, graduated with distinction. His mother has told several relatives that Vikram is "doing well." He knows the phrase was offered as a protective statement rather than a factual one. If his grades are poor, the cost will not remain his alone. It will be redistributed across conversations he will not hear, adjustments his parents will have to make in how they speak of their family. When the results appear, he does not look immediately. He waits until he is alone.

TRACE data show convergent gender patterns on izzat items in educational settings. Structural asymmetries persist outside education, where female responsibility is more often regulated through moral respectability and spatial restriction, whilst male responsibility centres on provider and status roles. Within the educational field, both genders share the logic of academic achievement as collective honour.

Izzat extends the frame of educational experience beyond the individual. Academic performance becomes a matter of shared reputation, embedding personal outcomes within a web of familial and communal standing that operates across time.

Izzat is the most expansive of the four concepts in its temporal and social reach. Where lajjā, vṛīḍā, and apatrāpya concern the individual's relation to moral norms, situational conduct, or social evaluation, izzat locates educational experience within a collective frame that extends across generations and social networks. Academic performance becomes not only personally significant but a matter of family and community standing, embedding individual outcomes in a web of shared reputation (Mathew & Pellissery, 2024; Gunasinghe et al., 2015, 2019). This collective embedding has a paradoxical structure: it intensifies the motivational stakes of academic engagement, binding success to a meaningful social project, and simultaneously amplifies the weight of failure, as individual shortfalls are experienced as threats to the honour of a wider group. Research on South Asian educational contexts consistently documents this dual dynamic, in which izzat functions not only as a restrictive social pressure but as a source of purpose and relational commitment (Mathew & Pellissery, 2024).

Inhibiting dimension

  • Collective honour framing amplifies individual shame through familial extension
  • Failure becomes a social event, not merely a personal one
  • Can restrict educational and career choices to honour-preserving options
  • Differentially constrains women's mobility and access outside the educational field

Resource dimension

  • Embeds academic effort in meaningful collective purpose
  • Generates sustained motivation grounded in relational commitment
  • Can build resilience through awareness of generational investment
  • Supports academic persistence under conditions of structural disadvantage
