Teaching in Shame-Sensitive Moments

A single laboratory session, read through the eight moments in which shame-sensitive teaching becomes concrete. Each tab highlights a different passage of the same scene and sets out what a response could protect or foreclose.

Vignette

Soma  ·  Second Year, B.Pharm  ·  Professor Singh It is a pharmaceutical analysis practical. Students work individually at their benches to determine the concentration of an unknown sample by titration. Soma transposes two values in her calculation, and Professor Singh, reviewing her notebook, points out precisely where her working departs from the correct method. Later, the group is asked to read out their results one by one, and Soma hesitates before her turn, aware that her value diverges noticeably from the range reported by her classmates. When Professor Singh asks why the discrepancy occurred, she cannot explain it and falls silent. Earlier in the session, she had noticed that her reagent was not behaving as expected. Rather than asking the laboratory assistant, she quietly changes her procedure, copying a technique she observes at the neighbouring bench without saying anything. A classmate notices and remarks, loud enough for others nearby to hear, that she does not seem able to follow the method properly. He adds, in passing, that this is probably what happens when someone's earlier schooling was not up to the standard expected here. After the session, Professor Singh asks Soma to stay behind briefly. Away from the group, he tells her that her documentation of the procedure was careful and thorough despite the miscalculation, and asks what made the reagent step difficult.

This vignette is an analytical composite informed by the empirical patterns described in Chapter 12.1 and by the open-text responses to item G6 in the TRACE study. It does not represent a single participant.

Eight moments of shame-sensitive teaching

12.1.1

Responding to Error

The correction stays attached to the calculation rather than to the person who made it.

Professor Singh, reviewing her notebook, points out precisely where her working departs from the correct method.

The correction itself is unremarkable. A transposed value is exactly the kind of error a titration calculation makes visible, and naming it clearly is part of ordinary academic feedback. What determines whether the moment becomes shame-sensitive is not whether the error is named, but where the naming locates the problem. Professor Singh directs attention to a specific step in the working, not to Soma's capacity to reason or to her place among students who are expected to succeed in the programme.

Normalisation is not the same as leniency. The academic standard does not soften because the feedback protects the person. Professor Singh can state exactly what the correct method requires while leaving the error open to revision rather than closing it into a verdict about ability. This distinction becomes more consequential the more a student already associates mistakes with doubts about belonging in the programme.

TRACE study, G6 response

"I want an atmosphere where mistakes are treated as opportunities to learn rather than reasons for embarrassment."

P304

Risk of an unreflected response

  • The error is framed as evidence of carelessness or weak ability rather than a step in reasoning
  • Correction becomes a judgement about the person instead of the working
  • Repeated exposure of this kind can encourage concealment rather than revision

Shame-sensitive practice

  • The problem is located in the argument, calculation, or method, not in the student
  • What the academic standard requires is stated clearly, without ambiguity
  • The error remains discussable and open to a next attempt

See Chapter 12.1.1 for the full discussion of responding to error, and Table 16 for its empirical grounding.

12.1.2

Public Exposure

Visibility is educationally ordinary and never emotionally neutral.

The group is asked to read out their results one by one, and Soma hesitates before her turn, aware that her value diverges noticeably from the range reported by her classmates.

Reading results aloud is a routine laboratory practice with a clear pedagogical purpose. It allows a class to compare methods and catch systematic error. For Soma, however, the same routine turns her deviating result into a public fact before she has had a chance to understand why it deviates. The hesitation before her turn registers this asymmetry, between a task designed for comparison and a moment that, for her, has become a test of standing among peers.

The practical does not need to be restructured to avoid visibility altogether. Smaller adjustments change what the same exercise asks of a student. Collecting results in writing before any are read aloud, allowing students to explain a deviation rather than merely report it, or opening the discussion with a commonly occurring source of error before individual values are named, all keep the comparative purpose of the exercise while reducing the likelihood that a single number becomes a public verdict on competence.

TRACE study, G6 response

"Teachers should not show disrespect in front of the class or other faculty members."

P425

Risk of an unreflected response

  • A deviating result is read aloud without context, becoming a public marker of failure
  • Comparison among peers substitutes for diagnosis of the underlying cause
  • Exposure carries greater weight for students whose belonging is already uncertain

Shame-sensitive practice

  • Results are collected or framed in ways that do not require immediate public comparison
  • Deviation is treated as a starting point for explanation rather than a conclusion about ability
  • Public correction remains proportionate to the actual learning purpose of the exercise

See Chapter 12.1.2 for the full discussion of public exposure in laboratory and presentation settings.

12.1.3

Supporting Not-Knowing

Silence is unreliable evidence of effort, motivation, or ability.

When Professor Singh asks why the discrepancy occurred, she cannot explain it and falls silent.

Soma's silence could be read in several ways, none of which are visible from the outside. It could reflect a genuine gap in understanding the titration method, a momentary loss of composure after the earlier correction, or uncertainty about whether an honest "I don't know why" will be treated as a reasonable answer in this room. Reading the silence too quickly as any single one of these forecloses the others.

A question can be reformulated, an intermediate step can be offered, or a partial explanation can be accepted as a starting point rather than a failure to answer. What matters most in this moment is time. Moving on to another student immediately can signal that Soma is no longer worth the instructional investment, even if that was not the intention. Holding the question open a little longer keeps the gap in knowledge separate from a judgement about her capacity to learn.

TRACE study, G6 response

"Give us space to express our thoughts without fear of being judged, and guide us patiently when we make mistakes."

P80

Risk of an unreflected response

  • Silence is interpreted as lack of preparation, motivation, or ability
  • The teacher moves on quickly, signalling that further attention is not warranted
  • The student experiences not-knowing as a fixed verdict rather than a temporary gap

Shame-sensitive practice

  • The question is reformulated, or an intermediate step is offered
  • A partial answer is accepted as a basis for further discussion
  • Wait time signals that the student remains a legitimate participant in the exchange

See Chapter 12.1.3 for the full discussion of supporting not-knowing and cognitive scaffolding.

12.1.4

Repair after Difficult Situations

Without repair, a brief incident can consolidate into a wider narrative of incapacity or exclusion.

Professor Singh asks Soma to stay behind briefly. Away from the group, he tells her that her documentation of the procedure was careful and thorough despite the miscalculation.

By the end of the session, Soma has been corrected, has hesitated publicly, has fallen silent under questioning, has quietly worked around a difficulty rather than asking for help, and has been the target of a dismissive remark linked to her schooling background. None of these moments was, on its own, extraordinary. Taken together, they could easily settle into a single reading of the afternoon, one in which Soma does not belong at the bench.

The brief exchange after the session interrupts that consolidation without pretending the difficulties did not occur. Professor Singh does not need to revisit each moment individually. Acknowledging something specific and true, that her documentation was careful despite the error, keeps the relationship and the possibility of continued participation open. Repair of this kind does not undo what happened. It prevents the afternoon from becoming the final word on Soma's place in the programme.

TRACE study, G6 response

"I would want a classroom atmosphere based on respect, empathy and encouragement, where students can take risks, learn openly and feel able to be themselves."

P309

Risk of an unreflected response

  • The session ends without acknowledgement, leaving the accumulated difficulty unaddressed
  • Several small incidents consolidate into a lasting impression of incapacity
  • The student is left to manage the emotional aftermath alone

Shame-sensitive practice

  • A brief, specific, and genuine acknowledgement follows a difficult session
  • The exchange takes place away from the audience that witnessed the difficulty
  • Renewed participation is made possible without requiring public re-exposure

See Chapter 12.1.4 for the full discussion of repair after difficult teaching situations.

12.1.5

Recognition in Teaching

Recognition confirms that a learner is entitled to participate, develop, and be taken seriously.

He asks what made the reagent step difficult.

The same brief conversation that repairs the session also does something distinct from repair. Asking what made the reagent step difficult treats Soma as someone whose account of her own difficulty is worth hearing, rather than as someone whose difficulty has already been fully explained by the miscalculation and the silence that followed. The question reopens a technical conversation on equal terms after a session that had, at several points, positioned her as the one who did not know.

Recognition of this kind extends beyond praise. Professor Singh does not need to overstate the quality of Soma's work to communicate that she remains a capable participant in the practical. Naming something specific, the documentation, and asking a real question about the difficulty she encountered, does more to confirm her standing than general encouragement would, because it treats her as someone with something accurate to report.

Risk of an unreflected response

  • The conversation ends with the error, without any acknowledgement of what the student did well
  • The student is left to conclude that the session confirmed a deficit rather than a difficulty
  • Recognition is withheld until performance is judged fully successful

Shame-sensitive practice

  • A specific strength is named alongside the acknowledgement of the error
  • The student's own account of the difficulty is invited and taken seriously
  • Recognition is offered as part of ongoing academic standing, not only after success

See Chapter 12.1.5 for the full discussion of recognition as a pedagogical intervention.

12.1.6

Help-Seeking

Asking for help has a social cost that the classroom itself can raise or lower.

Rather than asking the laboratory assistant, she quietly changes her procedure, copying a technique she observes at the neighbouring bench without saying anything.

Soma has a resource available to her, the laboratory assistant, and does not use it. Copying an unexplained technique from a neighbouring bench solves the immediate problem while leaving the underlying difficulty with the reagent unaddressed and unspoken. This is not independence. It is a workaround chosen because asking appeared to carry a greater cost than continuing without understanding why her own approach had stopped working.

Whether a question is perceived as a normal part of laboratory work or as an admission of insufficient preparation depends heavily on what students have observed happening to others who asked before them. Explicit invitations to raise a difficulty, treating a known point of confusion as expected rather than exceptional, and consistent patience towards students who ask more than once all lower the perceived cost of the request Soma did not make.

Risk of an unreflected response

  • Silence is mistaken for independence rather than avoided exposure
  • The underlying difficulty with the method remains unaddressed and may recur
  • Students learn to solve problems by imitation rather than by seeking clarification

Shame-sensitive practice

  • Questions are treated as an ordinary part of laboratory work, not as evidence of deficiency
  • Known points of difficulty are named in advance so a question confirms rather than exposes
  • Patience towards a student who asks repeatedly does not diminish with familiarity

See Chapter 12.1.6 for the full discussion of help-seeking and its social cost.

12.1.7

Shaming Behaviour

Some classroom conduct intensifies shame with little educational benefit, and silence toward it can read as permission.

A classmate notices and remarks, loud enough for others nearby to hear, that she does not seem able to follow the method properly.

The remark does not come from Professor Singh, but the practical continues under his supervision, and what he does next shapes its meaning. Left unaddressed, the comment can appear to have been permitted, effectively delegating the regulation of the room's tone to whichever student speaks loudest. Shaming behaviour is not only a matter of what teachers themselves say. It is also a matter of which peer conduct is allowed to stand unchallenged in a space the teacher is responsible for.

Addressing the remark does not require a confrontation that further isolates Soma. A brief, general statement about how the group discusses each other's work, made without singling her out further, can re-establish a norm without turning the correction itself into a second public moment centred on her. Leaving the comment unremarked is itself a decision, one that shifts the burden of objection onto the student who was targeted.

TRACE study, G6 response

"Teachers should discourage caste-based stereotypes and insensitive remarks, including subtle forms."

P294

Risk of an unreflected response

  • An unchallenged remark is read by the room as tacitly permitted
  • The affected student is left to manage the exposure without support
  • Repeated, unaddressed comments accumulate into an implicit classroom order

Shame-sensitive practice

  • A dismissive or mocking remark is named as such, without further exposing the student it targeted
  • A general norm for how the group discusses each other's work is restated
  • Repeated patterns of this kind are addressed directly rather than left to recur

See Chapter 12.1.7 for the full discussion of shaming behaviour in ordinary classroom routines.

12.1.8

Social Difference

An identical procedure can carry unequal risks depending on a student's social position and history of recognition.

He adds, in passing, that this is probably what happens when someone's earlier schooling was not up to the standard expected here.

The remark reframes a single technical difficulty as evidence about Soma's background, converting a laboratory error into a claim about where she comes from and what kind of education she is presumed to have had. This is not a comment about the titration. It draws on an assumption, widely available in Indian higher education, that unequal access to English-medium schooling, coaching, and preparatory resources reflects a difference in personal worth rather than a difference in prior opportunity.

Caste, language, regional background, and prior schooling should not be read as an adjustment problem belonging to the student who carries them. They are socially produced differences in risk, and an identical procedure, being asked to explain a deviating result, being expected to ask for help without hesitation, carries a heavier cost for a student whose belonging is already treated as conditional. Recognising this does not mean assuming a fixed meaning for any one background. It means not treating any single student's difficulty as representative of where they come from.

TRACE study, G6 response

"I would like an inclusive and respectful environment in the classroom, where students are not differentiated based on caste or background."

P3

Risk of an unreflected response

  • A technical difficulty is read as confirmation of a background-based assumption
  • Reserved-category status or regional origin is treated as evidence of lesser preparation
  • The same practice is experienced as low-stakes by some students and high-stakes by others

Shame-sensitive practice

  • A difficulty is treated as a difficulty, not as evidence about a student's origin or schooling
  • Comments that link ability to caste, region, or background are named and interrupted
  • Unequal starting conditions are considered before a gap is read as a difference in worth

See Chapter 12.1.8 for the full discussion of social difference and its bearing on apparently identical classroom practices.

Table 16

Empirically Grounded Pedagogical Responses

The eight moments read together, as they are set out in Chapter 12.1.

None of the eight moments in Soma's session operates in isolation. Cognitive shame about not knowing, the performative stakes of reading a result aloud, the relational meaning of a dismissive remark, and the structural weight of a comment about background all draw on the same underlying vulnerability, the risk that a temporary difficulty will be read as a permanent statement about who a student is and whether she belongs. Table 16 sets out the empirical starting point behind each response, the pedagogical response itself, and the rationale that connects them.

Empirical starting point Pedagogical response Rationale
Not knowing, grades, slower learning Framing not knowing as a temporary position, combined with appropriate cognitive support Protects the academic self-concept while keeping the learning task visible and achievable
Public exposure Careful structuring and moderation of situations involving public visibility Enables participation without allowing visibility to become a source of humiliation
Distance, impatience, rejection Patient, respectful, and responsive communication Sustains recognition and affirms the learner's legitimate position within the relationship
Reluctance to seek help Normalisation of questions and multiple routes to assistance Reduces the perceived status cost associated with help-seeking
Reflection and renewed effort Feedback that identifies an achievable next step Supports reflexive recontextualisation without encouraging global self-devaluation

Read as a sequence, the eight tabs also show that a single session offers repeated openings for the same underlying commitment. The teacher who corrects Soma's calculation without defining her by it, who slows down rather than moving past her silence, who addresses a peer's remark instead of leaving it to stand, and who takes a moment at the end of the session to ask what made the difficulty difficult, is applying one consistent orientation across several different moments, not several unrelated interventions.
