The TRACE study distinguishes seven categories of teacher interpersonal behaviour, three relationally burdensome, shaming and devaluing conduct, distance and non-recognition, and impatience in help-seeking, and four supportive, pedagogical responsiveness, emotional warmth, recognition and encouragement, and constructive feedback and respect. The vignette below follows Meera, a fifth-semester engineering student, across one semester and three of her teachers, since students themselves report on their experience across different teachers rather than a single relationship.

Vignette  ·  Meera, Fifth Semester, Computer Science

A semester across three teachers

Early in the term, Meera is asked by Dr. Verma to explain her results in front of the class. When she falters, he remarks that a fifth-semester student should not still be making this kind of error, and two classmates look down at their notebooks. Meera stops raising her hand in his sessions. Over the following weeks, she notices that Dr. Verma no longer looks towards her side of the room when he asks for volunteers. Uncertain about a concept before a deadline, she approaches him after class. He glances at his watch and tells her to look it up herself before wasting his time on something so basic.

Around the same time, in Dr. Kapoor's tutorial, Meera struggles with a recursion problem. Dr. Kapoor notices her hesitation and works through the base case with her step by step, without moving on until she nods. A few weeks later, in Professor Singh's lecture, he greets her by name as she enters and later makes the class laugh with a joke about his own coding mistakes from decades ago. When Meera contributes an unusual approach to a proof in a seminar, Dr. Kapoor tells the group that her method, though unconventional, deserves a closer look. Near the end of term, Dr. Kapoor reads through Meera's project submission carefully. He returns it with three specific suggestions for the following draft, and asks what she thinks of them before adding his own.

Burdensome

Shaming and Devaluing Behaviours

44.3% often or always criticised publicly 44.3% often or always experience disparaging remarks

Public criticism transforms error into social evaluation. What matters is not only the accuracy of the correction, but the audience before which it takes place.

Dr. Verma's remark to Meera takes place in front of the class rather than as a private exchange. Haas (2013) describes teaching as structurally organised as a public stage, on which correction delivered before an audience carries a particularly high potential for shame. Ruff (2024) frames such shaming as a deeply social event, one in which structured inequalities become operative in a concrete interaction, while Haas (2013) links it to a cycle of shaming in which teachers who lack strategies for processing their own past shame experiences are more prone to shame their students in turn.

Empirical finding

  • Responses to public criticism spread widely without a dominant tendency, suggesting individual teacher disposition rather than a shared norm
  • Disparaging remarks show the highest neutral share within this category, suggesting a situationally variable experience

Theoretical interpretation

  • Frey and Fisher (2008, cited in Stöhr & Schulze, 2024) identify sarcasm and reproach for poor grades as recurring themes in accounts of classroom shaming
  • Pedagogical tact, as Haas (2013) develops it, names the missing sensitivity for a moment's shame-inducing potential

See Chapter 7.3.5 for the full empirical account and theoretical discussion.

Burdensome

Distance, Unapproachability and Non-recognition

44.7% often or always experience distance 47.7% often or always feel ignored

Distance operates without a single dramatic incident. Its accumulation across ordinary interactions is what makes it relationally consequential.

Dr. Verma no longer looking towards Meera's side of the room is not an event she can point to, yet it communicates her standing as clearly as an explicit remark. Tormey (2021) distinguishes warmth, safety and power as dimensions of the affective teacher-student relationship, and locates unapproachability and non-response within the first two. The close correspondence between distance and non-recognition in the TRACE data suggests students experience these as one connected relational pattern rather than as separate behaviours.

Empirical finding

  • Nearly half of respondents describe both unapproachability and being ignored as recurring experiences
  • Both items show closely matching distributions, consistent with a single perceived relational pattern

Theoretical interpretation

  • Felten and Lambert (2020) describe relationship-rich education as built from repeated, ordinary accessibility rather than singular gestures
  • Gärtner and Sichler (2024) connect shame to being repeatedly overlooked, without any explicit shaming event

See Chapter 7.3.6 for the full empirical account and theoretical discussion.

Burdensome

Impatience and Lack of Willingness to Help

48.0% often or always experience impatience when seeking help Highest mean among burdensome behaviours

Help-seeking already requires a student to make her own uncertainty visible. Impatience in response raises the cost of that disclosure for every subsequent occasion.

Dr. Verma's dismissive response occurs privately rather than before an audience, yet it may shape whether Meera asks again. Academic help-seeking is understood as a core strategy of self-regulated learning, involving recognising a difficulty, assessing one's need, and deciding whether to ask (Ryan & Pintrich, 1997; Martín-Arbós et al., 2021). Herring and Walther (2016) show that engineering students in particular avoid help-seeking when questions might be read as a sign of insufficient competence, and Nathanson (2015) suggests impatience in response to disclosed vulnerability can intensify shame and entrench avoidance.

Empirical finding

  • Nearly one in two students reports impatience in help-seeking as a recurring experience
  • Only about a quarter report rarely or never encountering this behaviour

Theoretical interpretation

  • Martín-Arbós et al. (2021) note that the perceived cost of appearing less competent can deter help-seeking altogether
  • Disclosed vulnerability met with impatience can contribute to lasting avoidance of future help-seeking

See Chapter 7.3.7 for the full empirical account and theoretical discussion.

Supportive

Pedagogical Responsiveness

Understanding, patience and interest in wellbeing rated highest Noticing emotional distress rated lowest

Understanding for visible learning difficulties is experienced more consistently than the perception of unspoken emotional distress.

Dr. Kapoor's patient work through the base case of Meera's recursion problem exemplifies the most strongly endorsed behaviour in this category, patience with confusion. Di Lisio et al. (2025a) distinguish a Secure Base function that supports exploration from a Safe Haven function that stabilises students emotionally. The lower rating for noticing emotional distress points to a threshold that is harder to reach than responsiveness to visible academic struggle.

Empirical finding

  • Understanding, genuine interest in wellbeing, and patience with confusion all receive similarly strong endorsement
  • Noticing emotional distress falls markedly lower, without a clear positive majority

Theoretical interpretation

  • O'Brien (2023) distinguishes an assimilative mode of attention, fitting behaviour into existing categories, from a receptive mode requiring unhurried presence
  • Noticing unspoken distress requires the receptive mode and a relational continuity standardised teaching rarely supports (Noddings, 1992)

See Chapter 7.3.1 for the full empirical account and theoretical discussion.

Supportive

Emotional Warmth and Approachability

Smiling and humour rated most positively Sustained warm atmosphere rated lowest of the category

Group-directed, visibly enacted warmth is experienced more consistently than a sustained warm atmosphere or individually addressed accessibility.

Professor Singh's greeting and self-deprecating joke correspond to the two most strongly endorsed behaviours in this category, positive expression and appropriate humour, both public and group-directed. Vanderheiden et al. (2025) describe pedagogical warmth as grounded in unconditional acceptance and trust in student potential, extending beyond any single behaviour, while Hagenauer et al. (2023) identify accessibility as a defining feature of high-quality teacher-student relationships.

Empirical finding

  • Positive facial expression shows the strongest positive skew of the entire category
  • A consistently warm, welcoming atmosphere shows the highest negative share within the category

Theoretical interpretation

  • Nonverbal and atmospheric warmth appears to be experienced as punctual, visible practice rather than a continuously felt quality
  • Within the Guru-Shishya tradition, warmth has historically been transmitted collectively, yet in a status-asymmetric manner (see Section 8.2)

See Chapter 7.3.2 for the full empirical account and theoretical discussion.

Supportive

Recognition and Encouragement

Encouragement to ask questions rated highest Acknowledging imperfect contributions rated lowest

Encouragement to participate is experienced as comparatively stable practice. Acknowledgement of a contribution that remains imperfect is experienced as markedly less consistent.

Dr. Kapoor's response to Meera's unconventional proof, telling the group it deserves closer consideration despite its unfamiliarity, corresponds to the weakest-rated behaviour in this category. Honneth (1992) distinguishes love and care, legal rights, and solidarity as spheres of recognition. Encouragement to participate corresponds to legal recognition, requiring no evaluation of content, whereas acknowledging an imperfect contribution requires the more demanding solidary form.

Empirical finding

  • Encouragement to ask questions is the most strongly endorsed behaviour in this category
  • Acknowledging imperfect contributions is the only one without a clear positive majority, with the highest neutral share

Theoretical interpretation

  • Endedijk et al. (2022) describe the teacher's invisible hand as shaping acceptance hierarchies within the wider learning group
  • Tangney and Dearing (2002) and Nathanson (2015) describe anticipated non-recognition as a driver of withdrawal from visible participation

See Chapter 7.3.3 for the full empirical account and theoretical discussion.

Supportive

Constructive Feedback and Respect

Around a quarter of students remain neutral on both items Majority positive endorsement on both

Both behaviours signal whether students are perceived as independently thinking, opinion-holding subjects, rather than merely as recipients of correction.

Dr. Kapoor's handling of Meera's project submission, offering specific suggestions and asking for her view before adding his own, combines both dimensions of this category. Prengel (2013) argues for replacing negative comment with acknowledgement of what has been achieved, and introduces reversibility, the degree to which a teacher speaks in a tone a student could plausibly return, as a marker of respect for student opinion.

Empirical finding

  • Both feedback and respect fall in a moderate to slightly positive range, with notable neutral shares
  • Prengel's (2013) INTAKT project data found a positive outcome for about three quarters of observed classroom interactions

Theoretical interpretation

  • The elevated neutral share may reflect that teachers exercise discretion in feedback to differing degrees
  • Prengel (2013) notes that individual teachers vary considerably even within the same school

See Chapter 7.3.4 for the full empirical account and theoretical discussion.

Synthesis

Four Relational Mechanisms

The seven categories describe more than isolated teacher behaviours. They point to distinct relational processes through which the teaching relationship either supports or constrains shame-related learning experiences.

Mechanism 1

Perceiving the student

Understanding for visible learning difficulty is common. Noticing unspoken emotional distress is less reliably present.

Mechanism 2

Approaching the student

Group-directed, visible warmth is more consistently available than sustained individual accessibility.

Mechanism 3

Affirming the student

Encouragement to participate is stable. Recognition of imperfect contributions is less consistent.

Mechanism 4

Threatening the relationship

Shaming, distance and impatience are reported by substantial proportions of the sample as recurring experiences.

Across the supportive categories, behaviours that are visible, atmospherically present, and connected to already expressed needs receive the strongest endorsement. Less reliably present are behaviours requiring active perception of emotional distress, individually addressed attention, or continuous accessibility. Across the burdensome categories, behaviours with shame-inducing potential are reported by considerable proportions of the sample as frequent experiences, moving error into social visibility, weakening belonging, and making help-seeking harder to sustain precisely when support is needed.

The overall picture suggests a teaching environment marked not by a general absence of relational support, but by an uneven distribution of relational quality. General warmth and visible encouragement are experienced by many students, while the more demanding forms of relational engagement, perceiving emotional strain, recognising incomplete contributions, sustaining patience in help-seeking, appear less reliably present, though these are precisely the forms that help reduce shame-related withdrawal.
