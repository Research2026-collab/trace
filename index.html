Classroom Moment

Vignette

Professor Sharma pauses mid-lecture and calls on Arjun to complete the derivation on the board. Arjun rises, chalk in hand. He has studied the material, but standing at the board he cannot remember the next step. The room is silent. Thirty-two students watch. He writes one line, then stops, the chalk motionless. Professor Sharma says, quietly but clearly, "Anyone else?" and a second student walks up and completes it. Arjun returns to his seat. For the remainder of the lecture he does not raise his hand, though he knows two of the answers. At the break, his classmates gather near the window. No one comes to him. He takes out his phone. He thinks: next time, he will not risk being seen.

Dimension 1

Cognitive Shame

"I am not capable. This is who I am as a learner."

Cognitive shame operates as a global negative self-assessment that extends well beyond the immediate situation. When Arjun cannot recall the derivation, the experience does not register as a temporary gap in memory. It activates a deeper conviction about his intellectual inadequacy as a learner. The fact that he later withholds correct answers reveals the mechanism precisely: the shame is no longer tied to the board, but to his self-concept. Internalised performance norms, the expectation that a competent student answers correctly and immediately, have become the standard against which he measures himself, and against which he repeatedly falls short (Thornton et al., 2026; Zou et al., 2025).

Inhibiting dimension

  • Withdrawal from participation even when competent
  • Negative self-attribution becomes stable and self-confirming
  • Anticipated judgement replaces actual evaluation
  • Avoidance of help-seeking to protect self-image

Resource dimension

  • Signals a high internalised standard and motivation to close the gap
  • Can trigger self-reflection and deliberate study strategies
  • When reframed pedagogically, errors become epistemic events
  • Basis for a growth-oriented self-concept if supported relationally

See Chapter 4.1 for the theoretical foundations and TRACE empirical indicators of cognitive shame.

Dimension 2

Performative Shame

"Others have seen my failure. And now they know."

Performative shame emerges in precisely those moments of public visibility that are structurally built into most higher education classrooms: the oral response, the board work, the examination. What matters here is not whether Arjun ultimately knows the material, but that thirty-two peers and an authority figure have witnessed his standstill. The lecturer's quiet redirect, "Anyone else?", is not an aggressive act, yet it completes the public record of his failure. The discrepancy between Arjun's inner state and what the audience perceives is what Neckel (1993) describes as the "wound to the self": the exposure creates a meaning that outlasts the moment and affects the entire person, not merely the performance (Stöhr & Schulze, 2024; Wiesche, 2023).

Inhibiting dimension

  • Avoidance of future visibility in oral and public assessment formats
  • Freezing, blanking, and physical stress responses under observation
  • Shame becomes encoded as a feature of formal assessment contexts
  • Collective witness amplifies individual shame experience

Resource dimension

  • Public engagement builds tolerance for uncertainty and exposure
  • Peer witness of recovery can build mutual respect within a group
  • Scaffolded visibility formats reduce the stakes of any single moment
  • Mistakes normalised publicly can reduce cumulative shame load

See Chapter 4.2 for the theoretical foundations and TRACE empirical indicators of performative shame, including the role of izzat in collective honour exposure.

Dimension 3

Relational Shame

"I do not belong here. And they confirm it."

Relational shame concerns the affective consequences of social exclusion, marginalisation, or the withdrawal of recognition. In the vignette, the critical moment is not the board. It is the break. No classmate approaches Arjun. This is not necessarily an act of deliberate exclusion; it may simply be the ordinary social reorganisation of a peer group. Yet its effect is clear: Arjun registers his position as one of reduced belonging. His final decision, to avoid visibility next time, is not primarily about academic self-image or fear of public failure. It is a protective response to a perceived social reality: that being seen to fail changes where one stands in the group (Loveday, 2016; Sullins & Turner, 2024).

Inhibiting dimension

  • Social isolation following failure consolidates exclusion
  • Withdrawal from peer networks reduces access to informal learning
  • Threat to belonging activates disengagement from the institution
  • Chronic relational shame undermines long-term educational trajectories

Resource dimension

  • Strong relational need signals capacity for deep peer engagement
  • Peer solidarity after failure can transform shame into group cohesion
  • Belonging motivation supports sustained academic engagement
  • Relational sensitivity can support empathic classroom cultures

See Chapter 4.3 for the theoretical foundations and TRACE empirical indicators of relational shame, including patterns of social exclusion and peer dynamics.

Cross-Dimensional View

One moment, three processes

"The same situation activates different shame processes simultaneously. They reinforce each other."

Cognitive Performative Relational

The vignette now shows all three dimensions at once. Arjun's internal conviction of inadequacy (cognitive), the public record of his standstill (performative), and the social distance that follows at the break (relational) do not operate as separate episodes. They are simultaneous and mutually reinforcing processes arising from a single institutional event. The board work activates cognitive and performative shame in the same moment. The break activates relational shame as an aftermath. Arjun's decision not to raise his hand again is the product of all three processes acting together: a damaged self-concept, a fear of re-exposure, and a reduced sense of belonging. Any institutional response that addresses only one dimension, for example reforming assessment formats without attending to peer relations, will therefore be structurally insufficient.

This co-activation also carries resource potential. A single pedagogical intervention, for instance a lecturer who normalises the incomplete answer and invites the class to work through it collectively, can simultaneously reframe the cognitive meaning of the error, reduce the performative stakes of public exposure, and restore relational standing within the group. Not only are the three dimensions risks to be understood separately, but they are also points of access for integrated pedagogical change.

Integrative Model

The Triadic Self-Threat Model of Educational Shame

Cognitive, performative and relational shame converge in a single shame-related self-threat, shaped by the educational conditions in which learners are recognised, evaluated and positioned.

Arjun's episode can be read as one instance of a more general structure. Cognitive, performative and relational shame refer to threatened competence, threatened visibility and threatened belonging respectively, and converge in what the model terms shame-related self-threat. A learning situation becomes shame-relevant not because a difficulty, an exposure or an exclusion occurs in isolation, but because it acquires meaning for the learner's self-understanding, social worth and recognisability. The three dimensions are analytically distinct and empirically closely interwoven, since a single institutional moment, such as the board work described above, can activate all three at once.

Cognitive

Threatened competence

Performative

Threatened visibility

Relational

Threatened belonging

Whether shame is experienced at all, and how it is processed once it occurs, depends on mediating conditions surrounding the educational norms and recognition orders of a given setting. The model distinguishes five such conditions.

Pedagogical framing

Whether an error is treated as part of the learning process or as evidence of deficiency.

Relational recognition

Whether learners feel seen, addressed and taken seriously in their contributions.

Peer climate

Whether visibility among classmates is met with support, comparison, mockery or exclusion.

Institutional evaluation culture

The degree to which performance is formalised, ranked and translated into future opportunity.

Gendered expectations

How expectations regarding competence, self-presentation and belonging are differently applied.

Cultural meaning systems

Whether shame is understood as deficiency, moral sensor, social warning or occasion for reflection.

These conditions shape which of two processing modes shame-related self-threat takes.

Unmediated internalisation

Shame is taken up as evidence of personal inadequacy. The experience settles into the learner's self-understanding and can encourage withdrawal, avoidance, self-devaluation and non-learning.

Reflexive recontextualisation

Shame is read as a signal concerning norms, relationships and institutional conditions. The experience remains available for reflection rather than settling into self-devaluation, and can open a path towards personal and pedagogical change.
