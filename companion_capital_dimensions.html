Following Bourdieu (1986, 1992), the analytical framework applied throughout this book distinguishes six capital dimensions that structure access to, and experience within, Indian higher education. Cultural, educational, social, economic, linguistic, and symbolic capital accumulate, convert, and reinforce one another within institutional settings that have historically privileged particular social backgrounds, languages, and modes of conduct.

The vignette below is a composite portrait derived from the TRACE study data. It depicts a single institutional situation. Each capital dimension, accessible via the tabs, shows how that same situation is differently structured depending on which form of capital is under scrutiny.

Vignette  ·  Varun's Design Review

Varun is a second-year B.Tech student in Civil Engineering at a central university in Pune. He grew up in a small town in Uttar Pradesh; his father works as a government clerk, his mother did not complete secondary school. Varun passed the entrance examination on his third attempt. In a project review session, Professor Banerjee asks each student to present their structural load calculations on the whiteboard. Varun's calculations are correct. He has used an approach taught at his coaching centre, one that differs from the method introduced in lectures. When he presents, Professor Banerjee pauses, asks where he learned this method, and notes in front of the group that "this is not how we do things here." Two classmates who attended IIT-preparatory schools in Hyderabad exchange a glance. Varun completes the presentation. He does not ask for feedback. That evening, he searches online for the "correct" method and rewrites his notes. He does not mention the session to his parents when he calls home on Sunday.

Cultural Capital

University Habitus and Implicit Institutional Codes

Bourdieu, 1986  ·  Pichler & Prontera, 2012  ·  Müller, 2014

Manifestation Familiarity with academic conventions and implicit expectations Primary site Classroom interaction and pedagogical norms

In the vignette

Varun's calculations are correct. He has used an approach taught at his coaching centre, one that differs from the method introduced in lectures. When he presents, Professor Banerjee pauses, asks where he learned this method, and notes in front of the group that "this is not how we do things here." Varun completes the presentation. He does not ask for feedback. That evening, he searches online for the "correct" method and rewrites his notes.

Inhibiting dimension

  • Varun's coaching-centre method is technically valid, yet culturally illegitimate within the university's academic conventions
  • Professor Banerjee's public correction signals that belonging requires competence and familiarity with institutionally sanctioned modes of practice
  • Varun's self-directed correction that evening reproduces the institution's cultural authority without any formal instruction

Structural dimension

  • Students from urban, English-medium preparatory backgrounds enter already familiar with the university habitus. Varun must acquire it mid-programme
  • The institution does not make its cultural codes explicit. Their invisibility is precisely what makes them effective as mechanisms of differentiation
  • For policy: making implicit expectations visible in syllabi, induction programmes, and supervision practices redistributes cultural capital

Cultural capital manifests in what students know, in how they know it, and in how they perform that knowledge. The capacity to read and navigate implicit institutional expectations, what Bourdieu terms the academic habitus, is unevenly distributed along lines of social background, schooling biography, and geographic origin. Where this capacity is absent, even technically correct performance can be rendered socially insufficient.

Educational Capital

Recognised Qualifications and Institutional Performance Standards

Conrads et al., 2020  ·  Rafi et al., 2021

Manifestation Access to recognised credentials and institutionally legitimate assessment Primary site Examination, credentials, progression structures

In the vignette

Varun passed the entrance examination on his third attempt. His calculations are correct. He has used an approach taught at his coaching centre, one that differs from the method introduced in lectures. Professor Banerjee notes in front of the group that "this is not how we do things here."

Inhibiting dimension

  • Varun's three attempts at the entrance examination signal prior educational disadvantage that the institution does not address once he is enrolled
  • Assessment regimes reward familiarity with institutionally approved methods, not only technical correctness
  • The review session functions as an informal credential check, sorting students by their prior educational trajectories

Structural dimension

  • Entrance examinations concentrate prior educational advantage, admitting students who have already converted economic capital into coaching access
  • Once enrolled, students like Varun face assessment standards designed for those already familiar with the institutional method
  • For policy: diversifying assessment formats and making grading criteria transparent reduces the hidden advantage of prior educational capital

Educational capital operates through credentialled recognition of particular forms of knowledge. Access to recognised degrees and the institutional performance standards that govern academic careers reflects and reproduces the prior distribution of educational opportunity. Varun's correct, non-standard approach illustrates that academic assessment evaluates competence as defined by the institution's own historical standards, not competence in the abstract.

Social Capital

Mentorship, Informal Networks, and Relational Support Structures

Bihari, 2024  ·  Hunter, 2016  ·  Laishram & Haokip, 2023

Manifestation Integration into academic and professional networks Primary site Peer relations, supervision, informal academic life

In the vignette

Two classmates who attended IIT-preparatory schools in Hyderabad exchange a glance. Varun completes the presentation. He does not ask for feedback. He does not mention the session to his parents when he calls home on Sunday.

Inhibiting dimension

  • Varun has no access to the informal peer networks that would have prepared him for the institutional method before the review session
  • The exchanged glance between his classmates marks a relational boundary he does not cross
  • His decision not to mention the episode to his parents reflects the absence of familial social capital within the academic field

Structural dimension

  • Students from IIT-preparatory backgrounds enter with pre-formed peer networks and shared academic socialisation. Students like Varun must build networks from scratch in an unfamiliar environment
  • Mentorship relationships that facilitate orientation tend to form along lines of shared background and language
  • For policy: structured peer mentoring, especially across social backgrounds, actively redistributes social capital within the institution

Social capital is generated through mentorship, informal support, and integration into academic and professional networks. Its distribution follows the contours of shared educational biography, geographic origin, and social background. The informal knowledge that circulates within established peer networks, covering institutional methods, assessment expectations, and academic conduct, is structurally inaccessible to those excluded from those networks.

Economic Capital

Financial Resources, Income Stability, and Intergenerational Security

Goghari & Kusi, 2023  ·  Kumar et al., 2023  ·  Thorat et al., 2023

Manifestation Access to financial resources and opportunity structures Primary site Educational pathways, credential access, economic futures

In the vignette

His father works as a government clerk, his mother did not complete secondary school. Varun passed the entrance examination on his third attempt. Two classmates who attended IIT-preparatory schools in Hyderabad exchange a glance.

Inhibiting dimension

  • The family's economic position gave Varun access to a coaching centre, not to the IIT-preparatory institutions available to wealthier peers
  • Three entrance attempts represent three years of constrained economic opportunity and family investment under conditions of financial precarity
  • The correction in the review session reinforces the gap between Varun's educational trajectory and the institutional standard, with direct implications for progression and graduate employment

Structural dimension

  • Economic capital converts into educational capital through access to quality preparation; where that conversion is restricted, entrance examinations reproduce economic inequality
  • For students from OBC, SC, ST, and Dalit backgrounds, cumulative exclusions within academic institutions translate into restricted economic prospects despite formal inclusion
  • For policy: need-based financial support that extends beyond tuition, covering preparation, materials, and living costs, addresses economic capital deficits at the point of entry

Economic capital intersects with the other dimensions as an outcome of prolonged institutional positioning. Access to financial resources, stable income trajectories, and intergenerational economic security is shaped by educational pathways, credential recognition, and access to opportunity structures within higher education. For students from socially disadvantaged backgrounds, the relationship between educational and economic capital is cumulative and compounding across generations.

Linguistic Capital

Language Legitimacy and Standardised Academic Expression

Nasir, 2020  ·  Shakthi, 2024  ·  Yadav & Vanderheiden, 2026

Manifestation Familiarity with institutionally legitimate forms of language use Primary site Seminar participation, written work, informal academic interaction

In the vignette

Two classmates who attended IIT-preparatory schools in Hyderabad exchange a glance. Varun completes the presentation. He does not ask for feedback. That evening, he searches online for the "correct" method and rewrites his notes.

Inhibiting dimension

  • Varun's description of his method likely reflects a linguistic register acquired at the coaching centre, distinct from the technical academic language expected in the review session
  • Professor Banerjee's intervention, "this is not how we do things here," operates as a linguistic correction as much as a methodological one
  • Not asking for feedback after the session may reflect an awareness that the linguistic as well as the methodological gap is visible

Structural dimension

  • The institutional privileging of English and standardised technical vocabulary is historically linked to the colonial reorganisation of education and continues to distribute advantage along lines of schooling background
  • Familiarity with disciplinary language, covering the specific vocabulary, conventions, and genres of civil engineering discourse, is acquired through prior educational socialisation unavailable to Varun
  • For policy: explicit instruction in academic and disciplinary literacy, particularly for first-generation students, redistributes linguistic capital without lowering academic standards

Linguistic capital is embedded in educational inequality as part of the colonial reorganisation of the education system. The institutional privileging of English and standardised academic forms of expression is historically linked to processes in which language became a marker of educational legitimacy and authority. In contemporary higher education, familiarity with these linguistic requirements influences participation in seminars, examination performance, and informal academic interactions.

Symbolic Capital

Academic Legitimacy, Recognition, and Taken-for-granted Belonging

Lareau et al., 2018  ·  Bourdieu, 1992

Manifestation Recognition and credibility within the academic field Primary site Institutional interactions, peer relations, faculty evaluation

In the vignette

Professor Banerjee pauses, asks where he learned this method, and notes in front of the group that "this is not how we do things here." Two classmates who attended IIT-preparatory schools in Hyderabad exchange a glance. Varun completes the presentation. He does not ask for feedback.

Inhibiting dimension

  • Professor Banerjee's public correction withdraws symbolic recognition from Varun's competence. His technically correct answer is rendered symbolically illegitimate
  • The classmates' exchanged glance confirms a symbolic hierarchy that Varun's presence does not disrupt
  • Varun's silence after the session, not asking for feedback and not mentioning the episode to his parents, signals an internalisation of symbolic devaluation

Structural dimension

  • Symbolic capital operates through recognition, legitimacy, and attributions of belonging. Whose presence and competence are taken for granted, and whose educational aspirations are considered eligible for support, is structurally determined
  • Institutional practices that publicly mark deviation from the norm reinforce symbolic boundaries without requiring explicit exclusion
  • For policy: institutional recognition practices, including how faculty respond to non-standard approaches in public teaching settings, are a direct site of symbolic capital redistribution

Symbolic capital operates through recognition, legitimacy, and attributions of belonging, influencing whose presence and competence are taken for granted and whose educational aspirations are considered legitimate and eligible for support. In Varun's case, the withdrawal of symbolic recognition occurs through the ordinary pedagogical transaction of a review session, a moment that is structurally unremarkable to those who already possess it.
