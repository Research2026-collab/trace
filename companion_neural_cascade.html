What Happens in the Brain During Shame?

A single social-evaluative moment, followed through the four-step neural cascade described in Chapter 3.6, from alarm detection to cognitive regulation, grounded in the neuroscience developed across Chapter 3.2 to 3.5. A fifth step shows where the cascade leads, drawing on Chapter 3.7.

The cascade

Schematic side view. Positions are illustrative and follow the same simplified logic as Figures 2, 4 and 6 in Chapter 3, not a precise anatomical map.

Step 1

Alarm and Salience Detection

Before anything is consciously understood, the brain has already flagged the situation as socially dangerous.

Structures Amygdala, dACC, anterior insula Timing Pre-conscious, within milliseconds

A student is asked a question in front of the class and cannot answer. Before there is any conscious thought about the situation, the amygdala functions as a rapid subcortical alarm system, registering the moment as a threat to social standing (Ginot, 2024). In parallel, the salience network, particularly the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex and the anterior insula, flags the stimulus as highly relevant to social survival (Bastin et al., 2021). The dACC responds specifically to the anticipated social pain of devaluation or exclusion, drawing on neural pathways it shares with the processing of physical pain (Piretti et al., 2023).

This is not yet an emotion in the everyday sense. It is a rapid, embodied classification, dangerous or not dangerous, made before the situation has been thought through.

Cognitive resources begin shifting away from the task itself and towards threat defence, before the student has consciously decided anything (Amran & Bakar, 2020; Marks, 2013).

See Chapter 3.2 for the underlying neural networks and Chapter 3.6 for the cascade in full.

Step 2

Mental Representation and Self-Focus

The situation is now pictured, and attention turns from the outside world to the self as seen by others.

Structures DMN, PCC, precuneus, mPFC Timing Roughly 370 ms after the trigger

Electrophysiological studies show an early negativity in the cuneus around 370 milliseconds after a shame-related stimulus, thought to reflect the mental imaging of the social situation itself (Sánchez-García et al., 2021). At the same time, the brain recruits the Default Mode Network, particularly the posterior cingulate cortex and precuneus, to evaluate the self against social expectations, while the medial prefrontal cortex takes on the work of mentalising, estimating what others might now be thinking of the student (Takahashi et al., 2004; Bastin et al., 2021).

Attention has moved. It is no longer directed outward, at the question or the task, but inward, at a negative self-representation seen through an imagined audience (Roth et al., 2014).

This is the moment a factual difficulty, not knowing an answer, turns into a social one, being seen not knowing it.

See Chapter 3.2 for the Default Mode Network and Chapter 3.6 for the cascade in full.

Step 3

Visceral Response

Shame becomes a bodily event, not only a cognitive one.

Structures Anterior insula, autonomic nervous system Timing Roughly 700 ms after the trigger

The anterior insula processes uncomfortable internal signals, a sense of shrinking, tightness, heat, or nausea (Piretti et al., 2023). A shift from sympathetic towards parasympathetic dominance can trigger an impulse to disappear, expressed in lowering the head or blushing. A late positivity in the ERP pattern, appearing in medial parietal regions around 700 milliseconds, reflects the brain completing its calculation of social devaluation, comparing one's own conduct against internalised standards (Sánchez-García et al., 2021).

This is the stage that gives shame its distinctive bodily quality, the reason it is so often described in terms of heat, weight, or the wish to vanish, rather than in purely cognitive terms.

Participants in the TRACE body-mapping data locate these sensations most often in the face, chest and stomach, corresponding closely to this visceral stage of the cascade.

See Chapter 3.3 for shame as social pain and Chapter 6 for the empirical body-mapping data.

Step 4

Cognitive Regulation Attempt

The brain now tries to bring the intense affect back under control.

Structures Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex Function Cognitive control and affect regulation

The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex activates cognitive control mechanisms to regulate the intense negative affect and plan a socially acceptable response (Lloyd et al., 2021). Whether this succeeds is not guaranteed. The same stress response that triggered the alarm in Step 1 is, by this point, already drawing on the HPA axis and catecholamine release, which can themselves impair concentration, working memory and long-term memory retrieval (Amran & Bakar, 2020).

This is why a student who genuinely knows an answer can fail to retrieve it while standing at the board. The regulation attempt and the interference with memory draw on overlapping resources, and under sufficient load, regulation loses.

Intense shame can impair exactly the executive functions and memory processes a student would need to recover in the moment (Geldenhuys, 2019).

See Chapter 3.5 for the neurochemistry underlying this regulation attempt and Chapter 3.6 for the cascade in full.

Step 5

Where the Cascade Leads

A single episode is not yet a pattern. What happens after repeated activation is where the real stakes lie.

A single pass through this cascade is not, on its own, harmful. Shame is an adaptive signal, and the same system that produces an uncomfortable afternoon can also motivate reflection, repair, and renewed effort. What differs is what happens when this cascade activates repeatedly, over months or years, within a learning environment that offers little recognition or repair.

Adaptive pathway

Moderated by oxytocin and supported by recognition and relational repair, the same system sustains social bonds and moral integrity. Regulation succeeds often enough that shame remains a signal that can prompt reflection rather than a lasting injury (Zheng et al., 2024).

Chronic and toxic pathway

Repeated activation leads to sustained release of CRF, ACTH and cortisol, which impairs prefrontal neural proliferation and is associated with reduced PCC thickness and reduced right amygdala volume. Shame stops attaching to single situations and comes to characterise the self as a whole (Geldenhuys, 2019; Whittle et al., 2016).

Neuroscience can show which systems are involved in this cascade and what distinguishes its adaptive and chronic forms. It cannot, on its own, explain what a given moment of shame means to a particular student, in a particular classroom, within a particular cultural framework of recognition and belonging. That is the task of the chapters that follow.
